Ultrasound

Properties of US Beam

  • The area through which the sound energy is emitted from the US transducer travels is the US beam. US are sound waves whose frequency is \(> 2000 Hz\), sound waves of ranger 1 to 30 MHz are used in medical applications..

  • Beam Shape

    • The beam is three dimensional and is symmetrical around its central axis.
    • It can be divided into two regions: a near field (or Fresnel, pronounced fre-nel) zone) which is cylindrical in shape and a far field (or Fraunhofer zone) where it diverges and becomes cone-shaped.
    • The actual shape of the beam depends on the diameter of the crystal, the frequency and wavelength and the design of the transducer. Higher frequency waves correspond to longer Fresnel zone.

  • Beam Intensity: The intensity of the beam is the power flowing through a unit area. The intensity throughout the beam's length or width is not uniform.

  • US beams are non-ionizing and longitudinal.

Characteristics of Piezoelectric Crystal

The commonly used piezoelectric crystal in US transducers is PZT (Lead Zirconate Titanate), as these have strong response in transmission and reception. They can be formed in different shapes and sizes depending upon their applications. The transducer Q factor describes the shape of the frequency response curve and is defined as \[Q = \frac{f_o}{f_2 - f_1} \] Clearly, the transducer Q factor is determined by two characteristics of the piezoelectric crystal, frequency and bandwidth.

Curie temperature is the temperature beyond which a crystal loses its piezoelectricity.

Resonant frequency of the piezoelectric crystal is determined by the thickness of the crystal. Resonance becomes maximum when the frequency of the crystal matches its natural/resonant frequency.

Ultrasound Transducer

An ultrasound transducer is a device that converts electrical energy into mechanical energy (acoustic energy) and vice versa. So , the ultrasound transducers serve two functions in ultrasonography:

  1. It converts the electrical energy provided by a transmitter into acoustic pulses.

  2. The transducer also serves as the receiver of reflected echoes, converting weak pressure changes into electric signals.

Transducer components

  1. Piezoelectric crystal (PZT)
  2. Matching layer
  3. Damping layer
  4. acoustic insulator
  5. Metal shield
  6. Plastic housing
  7. Transducer leads (to transmitter)

Pulse Echo Principle

In most clinical applications, a pulsed ultrasound is used, in which brief bursts of acoustic energy are transmitted into the body. The ultrasound transducer is energized by application of a precisely timed, high-amplitude voltage. The time interval between the ultrasound pulses is controlled by the transducer.

In pulse echo method, a short ultrasound pulse is emitted from the transducer. This pulse travels along a beam pointing in a given direction. The ultrasound beams are parallel at the surface of the transducer and start livering at a distance from the transducer (Fresnel and Fraunhofer zones). The us beam penetrates through the superficial and get reflected back after striking a structure. This reflected signal is is referred to as the echo signal or the received signal. The echoes generated by the pulse are recorded by the transducer. The later an echo is received, the deeper is the location of the structure giving rise to the echo. Depth resolution is superior with us beam that have a longer Fresnel zone.

In addition to that, the amplitude of the echo signal is proportional to the average specific acoustic impedance difference between the structure and the tissue just above. An image is then created by repeating this process with the beam scanning the tissue.

Ultrasound Modes

Amplitude Mode (A mode)

The A mode is only used in ophthalmology to view the optic never. In this mode, the echoes are displayed as spikes projecting from a base line.

Brightness Modulation (B Mode))

The B mode is the most common mode used in ultrasonography. Unlike A mode, B mode does not have vertical spikes. The image brightness depends on the amplitude or intensity of the echo. B mode displayers images as large and small dots, which represents citron and weak echoes respectively.

Motion Mode (M Mode)

This mode is also referred as the time-motion (or TM) mode. It is used to image one dimensional structures, that is uid for analyzing movement of body parts. For example, for imaging blood flow in blood vessel (one-dimensional). This is mostly common in cardiac and foetal cardiac imaging.

This is accomplished by recording the amplitude and the rate of motion in real time by repeatedly measuring the distance of the object form the sidle transducer at a given moment.

Real Time Imaging

Real time imaging systems are those that have frame rates fast enough to allow movement to be followed. A real time ultrasound transducer can produce multiple frames in a very short time typically at least 10 frames per second.

Image Quality in Ultrasound

The key factors that determine the image quality of an ultrasound image includes spatial, contrast and temporal resolution.

  1. Spatial Resolution must be considered in 3 planes and there are different determinants of resolution in each of these.

    • Axial or depth resolution: this is the ability of US beam to separate two objects lying in tandem along the axis of the beam

    • Lateral or horizontal resolution is the ability of the US beam to separate two adjacent tissues (or objects). It refers to resolution in the plane perpendicular to the beam and parallel to the transducer. The lateral resolution is determined by the width of the beam.

    • Azimuth or Elevation resolution is the slice thickness in the plane perpendicular to the beam and to the transducer. It is determined by the construction of the transducer and generally, it cannot be controlled.

Artifacts in Ultrasound

Different type of artifacts in ultrasound are as follows:

  • Reverberation Artifact: This arises when the ultrasound signal reflects repeatedly between highly reflective interfaces that are usually near the transducer. It may also give a false impression of solid structures in areas where only fluid is present. To minimize reverberation artefact, the transducer placement i.e. the scanning angle must be changed.

  • Side lobe artifact: Although most of the energy transmitted by the transducer is confined at (emitted along) the center of the US beam, some energy is also emitted at the periphery of the primary beam. These energy (at the periphery) are called side lobes and have a lower intensity than the primary beam. However, these side lobes may interact with strong reflectors that lie outside the scan plane and produce artifacts.

  • Shadowing: Shadows may result when an object attenuates sound more rapidly than surrounding tissues.

  • Vanishing twin artifact: The refraction of ultrasound from supra-pubic regions may produce double outline of pelvic structures or may even produce double structures themselves, which hides one of the embryo in early pregnancy.

  • Comet tail artifact is produced due to large number of bubbles present in the bowel gas.

  • Mirror image artifact is normally seen at the air-pleura interface above the diaphragm. It occurs when the reflector lies at some angle to the US beam. US is strikes the tissue at an angle and is reflected back to the transducer (after multiple reflections) which gives an impression as being seen from a mirror.

  • Increase through transmission: This artifact is associated with attenuation errors. When the US beam encounters weakly attenuating structure, the amplitude of the beam beyond this structure is greater than the beam amplitude at the same depth in the rest of the field. The echoes returning from structures deep to the weak attenuator will be of higher amplitude and will be falsely displayed as a bright band extending from an object.

Interaction of US with matter

A beam of ultrasound may interact with matter in 5 possible ways - reflection, scattering, refraction, absorption and attenuation. Here's a diagrammatic representation of how these look:

The US image is produced by the reflected portion of the originally transmitted beam. There are two factors the influence reflection:

  • The acoustic impedance between tissues
  • The beam angle i.e. the placement of ultrasound transducer
In ultrasound, there can be two types of reflections \(-\) Specular and diffuse reflections. If the reflecting interface is large and smooth, it reflects sound as much as a mirror reflects light. Such interfaces are called specular interfaces and such reflections are referred to as specular reflections. On the other hand, if the reflecting interface is rough and has smaller structures with individual dimensions smaller than the wavelength of incident sound then the echoes from these interface undergo scattering in all directions and only some of the incident rays are reflected back. These reflectors are called diffuse reflectors and the phenomenon is called diffuse reflection.

Refraction occurs when sound passes through a tissue with one acoustic propagation velocity to another tissue with a different acoustic propagation velocity. Knowing the refraction index allows operators to adjust the beam angle so that it is perpendicular to the interface, which inturn minimizes the artifacts seen on the image.

Another way that an US beam interacts with matter is absorption. The intensity of the ultrasound beam falls off rapidly as it propagates through soft tissues. This is the most dominant cause of energy loss, especially in soft tissues. The frequency of sound and the viscosity of the medium determine the amount of absorption.

Scattering occurs, as suggested above, when the sound strikes a rough surface. The amount of scatter depends on the dimensions of the individual structures present on the surface. The US beam does not loose any energy when it is scattered, it just changes the direction of propagation. Generally, the energy which does not interact with the target is redirected in almost all directions. For instance, US beams encountering blood are scattered in all directions.

Attenuation isn't exactly interaction of sound with matter, rather it is the result of other interactions. A sound passes through the tissues, it loses energy through transfer of energy to tissue by absorption, scattering, and refraction. The energy is lost in the form of heat and this loss of energy is termed as "attenuation". Attenuation is determined by

  • The resonating frequency: attenuation increases in proportion to resonating frequency, resulting in less penetration at higher frequencies. (Isn't this in contrast with the Fresnel zone logic??)
  • The nature of the attenuation medium: Attentuation values for normal tissues have considerable deviations. For example, attenutation value for water 0.00 and for air, it is 10.

Doppler Ultrasound

Principle

The doppler ultrasound depends on the principle of Doppler effect. The doppler effect is the change in perceived frequency of sound emitted by a moving source.

Reasons why Ultrasonography is popular in Medical Applications

  • No ionizing radiation
  • Opts for real time imaging using doppler ultrasound
  • Can be done in almost all type of patients
  • Lower cost
  • Lower time
  • Portable and easily accessible
