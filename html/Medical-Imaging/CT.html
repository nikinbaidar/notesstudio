Computed Tomography CT

A computed tomography is an imaging technique that involves capturing multiple projections of a 3D object and reconstucting the image of that object from those projections using complex mathematical algorithms.

The basic principle of CT is that the internal structure of an object can be reconstructed form multiple projections of that object

CT Generations

First Gen CT

  • X-ray beam was collimated to a (pencil width) narrow beam and was detected by a single detector.

  • The xray tube and the scintillation detectors were rigidly linked (fixed).

  • Starting at a particular angle, the x-ray tube and the detector system translated linealy across the FOV acquiring 160 parallel rays per view.

  • After each translation cycle, x-ray tube and the detectors rotated by 1o until path length of 180o<\sup> was covered.

  • In this tranlate-rotate motion, a total of 180 x 160 = 28,800 projections were made.

  • These were specifically designed for imaging the internal strucutures of the skull.

  • The advantage of this system was there was minimum scatter raditation. But the major drawback was the duration (around 5 mins) that it took to scan a single image. Furthemore, the contrast resolution of internal structures was unprecedented and images had poor spatial resoultion.

Second Gen CT

  • Instead of a pencil beam, like in the first gen ct, a fan beam of around 3 to 10 degress were used. And the rotation angle was also increased by ten folds to the first gen ct.

  • The number of detectors were also increased up to 30 detectors.

  • With 10 degree rotation increment, only 18 translations were required for a 180o image acquisition. This change only made second gen cts x15 faster compared to the fitst gen.

  • The disadvantage of the second gen ct was that the complexity of the (translate-rotate) system prevented it from being further improved.

  • Even a small deviations in milimeters of the scanner hardware position, relateive to the reconstruction voxels would cause data to be back projected through wrong voxels, creating several artefacts.

  • The fan beam used in the second gen ct, although it covered bore area, had the disadvantage that the radiation intensity would increase toward the edge. A bow-tie filter could be used to minimize this but it would also limi the range of intensity reaching detector and hardens the beam.

Third Gen Ct

  • The third gen ct completely discarded the translate-rotate tube-detector system of the first and the second gen ct and opted for a full rotational mechanism.

  • It used wide angle fan beams of around 50 to 55 degress and the number of detectors was increased to more thean 800 detectors. The x-ray tube was the apex of the fan beam.

  • This sort of arrangement allowed third generation ct scanners to make a scan in a matter of a few seconds.

  • The primary disadvantage of this system was that the use of larger number of detectors affected the spatial resoultion up to a certain degree. Furthermore, the detector array required high stablility. Any error or drift in the calibration of the detectors relative to other detectors would lead to back projectoins along the ray and path and reinforcement along a ring where they cross. This would result in an artefact known as the ring artefact.

  • Ring artefacts are never completely eliminated, rahter minimized by high quality detectors design and frequent calibration. Residual righ artifacts are removed by image processing algorithms.

  • Another disadvantage arised in sampling. The sample size and spacing were fixed by the detector desing. Samples cannot be closer than the distance between rays associated with the detector at the lever of center of rotation.

Fourth Gen CT

  • Fourth generation ct scanner employed a large number of stationary ring of detectors and a rotating xray tube. Meaning only, the x-ray tube would rotate around the patient.

  • It employed round 4800 individual detector and spatial resolution of moer than 20 lp/cm. Each individual detector was the apex of the fan beam used in 4 th gen ct which is in contrast with the 3 rd gen.

  • The scan time in fourth generation ct was a fraction of a second.

  • The disadvantage of fourth gen ct was that becuase the tube rotated inside the detectory ring, large ring diameter was needed. On the other hand, acceptable spatial resolution limited the detector aperture to approximately 4mm.

  • Scatter radiation was more because the septa used in the 3 rd generation of ct could not be used as the septa needed to be aimed at the center of the ring.

Fifth Gen CT

  • Used for cardiac imaging. Previous generations of CT could not be used for cardiac imaging because it required ultra fast scanning times \(<50ms\) which was difficult to attain.

  • Scan time in 5\(^{th}\) gen ct was as little as 10 to 20 ms. Such little scan time was possible through use of a large bell shaped x-ray tube rather than conventional X-ray tube. A large arc of tungsten encircles the patient ad lies directly opposite to the detector ring.

  • An electron beams produced from an electron gun placed behind the gantry strikes tungsten arc. The electron beam is produced in cone like structures and are electronically steered around the patient so that it strikes the target. X-ray are produced when the electrons strike the tungsten target. This concept is knows as the Electron beam CT.

  • EBCT is limited only to cardiac imaging due to the image quality for general screening is lower than of conventional CT and also due to high equipment costs.

  • Cardiac scanning mostly is done by multislice CT, so the future of EBCT is rather uncertain.
