Limitations of Film Screen System

  • Exposure errors are permanent on image
  • Faults in the film requires the examination to repeated.
  • Delay in viewing the image.
  • The dynamic range of the X-ray film is limited.
  • Difficult to visualize widely varying tissue densities
  • Storage and retrieval costs
  • Copied radiographs are of inferior quality compared to the original
  • Procedural Issues

Digital Radiography overcomes limitations of Film Screen System

  • Eliminate film processing, no dark room or equipment required, no film file room.
  • Eliminates the necessity for retakes as errors can be fixed using software.
  • Increased throughput
  • Easier image archiving, retrieving and transmission.
  • Vastly higher image dynamic ranger- wider exposure and higher image SNR (contrast).
  • Image is formed almost immediately
  • Various image parameters such as contrast, brightness etc can be enhanced using software.
  • Potentially lower dosage.

Despite all of these advantages, a major disadvantage of digital radiography is that it has lower spatial resolution compared to conventional screen film system.

DR: Physical Principles

Digital detectors are used to generate a digital radiograph. The detector is exposed to x-rays, and the energy absorbed by the detector is transformed into electrical signal either directly (direct radiography) or indirectly (computed or scanned projection radiography). The electrical signal is recorded, digitized and quantified into a gray scale image. Finally, post processing of raw data into a clinically meaningful image is performed.

Types of DR

DR can be classified into the following categories:

  • Scanned Projection Radiography (SPR)
  • Computed Radiography
  • Direct Radiography (DR) (This bits confusing)

SPR was developed as an offshoot of CT. Computed radiography isn't exactly a digital radiography. Rather it bridges the gap between conventional radiography and digital radiography. The image acquisition part of CR is quite similar to conventional screen film system except it does not use intensifying screens and employs a PSP image plate as detector. The final image is digital nonetheless. DR (Direct Radiography) is in its perfect sense a digital radiography. DR system uses detectors which convert x-rays into electrical charges by means of direct readout process.

CR uses PSP plates as the detector, which needs to undergo a readout process before an image is formed. Direct radiography uses flat panel detectors that do not require to processed for image formation.

Computed Radiography

As mentioned earlier, CR is not a pure digital radiographic system. It uses photo stimulable phosphor (PSP) plates as the detector. These use Eu:BaFX. During exposure, x-ray energy is absorbed temporarily in the PSP crystal by exciting electrons to higher energy levels. A separate readout process is employed to convert the acquired image into a digital image.

PSP Readout

The exposed cassette is moved onto a reader unit where the IP is mechanically removed from the cassette and scanned pixel by pixel by a laser beam. The laser light stimulates emission of trapped energy in the imaging plate and a visible light is emitted from the imaging plate. The released light is collected by a fiber optics cable that guides light to a PMT (Photomultiplier tube) where it is converted into an electronic signal.

Direct Radiography

Direct radiography is a system in which no cassettes are used, instead a large area detector panel is used to capture the x-rays and convert them into digital data for subsequent display of image. Now a days, a cassette like DR panel without a cable connection is also in use. DR is extremely fast in terms of image display and is more convenient as the need to carry the cassettes from one place to another is eliminated.

Direct radiography itself is of two types: Indirect DR and Direct DR.

  1. Indirect (Direct Radiography): Direct in the sense that there is no need to undergo an image formation process like in CR or screen film system and indirect in the sense that the x-ray photons first need to be converted into light photons before they can be converted into electrical signals. Indirect DR can be further divided into two types based on the detectors used as CCD panel based indirect DR and Flat panel based indirect DR.

    Both types of indirect DRs use CsI of GdOS scintillator detectors, although nowadays GdOS are used extensively. CsI phosphors used in indirect DRs have high detector quantum efficiency (DQE) which opts for lower patient dosage. \[ DQE = \% \text{of X-ray absorbed by the phosphors}\]

  2. Direct (Direct Radiography): In direct DR, a photoconductor, selenium directly converts the x-ray photons into electrical signal. It used flat panel detectors.

    DDR uses a-Se only which eliminates spreading of light in the phosphor which opts for better spatial resolution.

Charged Coupled Devices (CCDs) and CCD based Indirect DR

CCDs are photosensitive silicon chips about \( 1/6''\) to \( 1.3''\) square size so several may chips may be coupled to cover a required FOV. CCDs convert light into electronic signals; when the silicon layer in the CCD is illuminated and electric charge is generated. The charge accumulate in individual pixels cells. The accumulated charge is read out pixel by pixel. Each CCD chip has a discrete pixel electronics etched into its surface, for instance a \( 2.5x2.5 cm\) CCD chip may have \( 1024x1024\) or \( 2048x2048\) pixels on its surface.

In a CCD based indirect DR, the imaging device is a panel consisting of a layer of scintillator material such as CsI (not these days) or GadOS) above the array of CCDs. CCD based DR panels that has a of the dimensions \( 59x48x6 cm^3 \) consists of 240 CCDs and provides \( 60.45 \) megapixels image quality.

Flat Panel Detectors and FPD based Indirect DR

The technology employed in flat panel detectors is quite analogous to that used in laptop computer displays; a series of horizontal and vertical electrical lines are used which when combined with appropriate readout logic can address each individual display element. With this approach only 2,000 connections between the detector panel and the readout electronics are required for a \( 1000x1000\) display instead of 1,000,000 individual connections.

In FPD based indirect DR, a CsI or \( Gd_2O_2S\) scintillation phosphor is coated over an active matrix array (AMA) of a-Si TFTs. The DR image receptor is fabricated into individual pixels and each pixel has a light sensitive face of a-Si, a capacitor and a TFT embedded into it.

Thin Film Transistors (TFTs)

TFT arrays are used as the active electronic element in both direct and indirect DRs based of FPDs. TFT arrays are typically deposited onto a glass substrate in multiple layers, beginning with readout electronics at the lower levels and followed by charge collector arrays at higher levels. X-ray elements, light sensitive elements or both are deposited to form the topmost layer of this electronic sandwich. The entire assembly is encased in a protective enclosure with the external cabling for computer connection.

Readout Process of TFTs

TFTs can be read either directly or indirectly. In FPD based DDR, the image forming x-rays directly interacts with a thing layer of a-Se creating an electron-hole pair. a-Se of about \( 200 \mu m\) is sandwiched between charged electrodes. The electron hole-pair is the signal that charges the AMA of TFTs.
